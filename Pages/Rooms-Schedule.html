The idea

A retreat house changes the whole weekend.

A hotel program gives you a room and a schedule. This is different. The house is the center of the weekend: meals in one place, slow conversations after meals, games that start naturally, and a group that gets to actually share space.

We’re building the retreat around that feeling — enough structure to make Shavuos meaningful, enough softness to let people breathe, and enough shared living space to make connection feel real.

The rooms matter, but the common spaces matter more. The retreat is designed around the dining room, lounge, game room, pool, and those in-between moments that make the weekend feel lived in.

The application process helps us shape a group that feels warm, intentional, and comfortable. We’re not trying to fill a hotel; we’re trying to build the right room.

The rhythm should feel full but not overwhelming — Yom Tov, rest, meals, games, fresh air, a few memorable moments, and space to actually enjoy the place.

Rooms are not the product.

They are the base. The real experience is the shared house rhythm: who you meet, where you sit, what happens after meals, and how the whole place feels by the second day.

Schedule, but with air.

We’ll have structure, but we’re not publishing every mechanism. Some parts should stay alive, flexible, and shaped by the people who come.

Vermont is part of it.

The mountain setting, slower pace, and private-home feeling are not background details. They’re part of why this retreat should feel different.
